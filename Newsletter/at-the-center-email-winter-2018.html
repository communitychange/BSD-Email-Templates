 In These Times

A  Message from Deepak Bhargava


We all remember where we were when the twin towers of the World Trade Center fell and the Challenger space shuttle exploded. We remember these moments in our lives—the rooms we were in, the people by our side—because of their significance. There is a before and a sharply distinct after.

In our country’s story, 1968 holds the same significance. The assassinations of the Rev. Dr. Martin Luther King, Jr. and Sen. Robert F. Kennedy left heartbreak in their wake, but also a yearning and belief that we can and must do better for our families and ourselves.

The country was in conflict over a war in Vietnam and the rights of black Americans. It was fighting for its very soul. In that movement moment, people of all backgrounds—across class and race and gender—stepped into the streets. They marched and they organized. They spoke out and they voted. As the War on Poverty came to an end and Richard Nixon won the White House, they kept striving towards a world where everyone thrives. The Center for Community Change (CCC) was born into this world on fire.

Safety Net:In 2017, we successfully defended safety-net programs, working in coalition with a broad set of partners from coast to coast. But despite the extraordinary feat of preserving the Affordable Care Act and preventing the dismantling of Medicaid, our fight isn’t over. Fifty years after we took on hunger and reshaped the modern food stamps program, CCC is once again fighting for programs that provide the basic dignities of putting food on the table, seeing a doctor, living independently ...

Immigration:The rollercoaster of emotions for Dreamers has not slowed down in 2018. In February, Congress once again failed to pass meaningful legislation to create a path to citizenship for immigrant youth who were brought here as children and afforded legal status under the Deferred Action for Childhood Arrivals Act, as well as others in the immigrant community. We are outraged. Too many elected officials continue to support President Trump’s racist views on immigration, leaving millions in limbo without a permanent legislative solution. Our resolve, however, remains strong ...

With help from CCC, FIRM group CHIRLA posted a chilling and impactful portrayal of the reality immigrants face on a daily basis from ICE and in detention in a new Know Your Rights video, America, Yo Tambien. Board Member Manuel Pastor in the NYT
CCC’s board member Manuel Pastor was quoted in The New York Times for a story entitled Immigrant Shock: Can California Predict the Nation’s Future? In the piece, Pastor, a USC sociologist, recounts California’s history with immigration and explains how its demographic change between 1980 and 2000 mirrors the changing U.S. population between 2000 and 2050, when white people are expected to be less than half of the nation’s population. The question, he says, is whether the rest of the country can adjust faster to demographic change ...
